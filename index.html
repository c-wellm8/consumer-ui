Healthspan vs Lifespan

Living longer isn't the same as living well longer. Lifespan is how many years you live. Healthspan is how many of those years you spend in good health — active, independent, doing the things you care about.

The gap between the two has been growing. In the US, the average person lives to about 77, but only about 63 of those years are spent in good health. That's a 14-year gap where chronic conditions take over.

Age Well tracks both: life-minutes show how your choices extend your time, and quality-minutes show how they protect the quality of that time. The goal isn't just more years. It's more good years.

You have more power than you think

Most people believe longevity is mostly genetic — you either got lucky or you didn't. The research tells a very different story.

Large twin studies and a major analysis of over 400 million family trees found that only about 20–25% of how long you live is explained by your genes. The rest — roughly 75–80% — comes down to what you do every day: how you move, what you eat, how you sleep, who you connect with, and how you handle stress.

None of this requires extreme measures. Consistency in the basics — a daily walk, real food, decent sleep, a conversation with someone who matters — is what the evidence actually rewards.

How small actions add up

A 30-minute walk doesn't feel like a major health decision. But at the population level, regular moderate movement reduces all-cause mortality risk by roughly 15–25%.

Age Well translates that into something you can feel: about 9 extra life-minutes per day from that one habit. That's around 55 hours per year. Over a decade, roughly 23 extra days of life — from a single daily walk.

Now layer on nutrition, sleep, connection, stress management. The math compounds. Age Well makes this visible: not as a promise or prediction, but as an approximate reflection of what the evidence says your daily choices are actually worth.
